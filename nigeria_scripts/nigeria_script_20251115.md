The intention is there—to cool the heat on prices—but in practice, it trickles down slow, like water from a cracked bucket. Families still stretch every kobo at the end of the month, kids dreaming of better tomorrows while parents pray for provision. You know that story of the widow's mite? It's us, giving our all in faith, trusting that grace will multiply what's little into abundance.

That Anambra election neutrality our president maintained? The ruling party praises him for staying out, like a referee who doesn't pick sides in a heated match. Neutrality sounds fair, but in our heated political arena, it's rarer than finding a quiet corner in a bustling market. Everyone's pulling strings, from Igbos in the city claiming the union is illusory to whispers of lopsided developments favoring one spot over others. Is the top man leading the whole nation or just his backyard? Questions swirl like dust in harmattan wind. Yet, amid the noise, we Nigerians keep pushing—extraordinary teens inventing solutions from scraps, drummers confessing the rhythm of survival, ranking our staples as if food wars can distract from bigger battles. The condition we face? Challenged coexistence, as one leader put it, but laced with that unbreakable spirit.

But through it all, there's that quiet faith—prayers in midnight hours, choruses of hope in churches packed tighter than buses during rush. The Americans and our folks collaborate on fixes, but progress crawls slower than a loaded truck on bad roads. Sulk? No, we spice it with sarcasm, turning pain into punchlines.
