And our side doesn't just sit quiet; no, they call in every embassy boss in the capital for a serious sit-down, like it's family meeting time over jollof rice gone wrong. Shey you see the drama? One side flexing muscles from across the ocean, the other rolling out the red carpet for talks, assuring everyone that peace is the plan. But deep down, we all know how these international wahala go: plenty noise, zero action, and by next week, it's back to our own daily grind of dodging potholes and bargaining at the market.

That reminds me of how quickly our leaders pivot when the heat is on, abi? Take this recent flip on that import charge for petroleum products, the one that was set at fifteen percent and now suddenly it's off the table. Like, yesterday they announce it with fanfare, as if it's the solution to every empty pocket, and today? Poof, U-turn faster than a danfo driver spotting a traffic warden. I laugh because it's like watching your uncle promise to fix the roof before the rains, then when the sky opens, he says, "Ehen, let's pray instead." You understand? These policy somersaults keep us on our toes, wondering if tomorrow they'll bring it back or invent something new. And the ordinary person? We're left holding the bag, calculating how much more we'll pay at the pump or the stove, while the big shots pat themselves on the back for "listening to the people." Listening, sha, but maybe only the echoes in their own halls.

One day you're red, the next you're green, and suddenly everyone's hailing it as a masterstroke for unity. Me, I dey think: is this politics or market bargaining, where you haggle over prices until you join the higher bidder? But honestly, in our setup, these jumps happen so often, it's like changing shirts after a sweaty football match. Does it fix the roads, the schools, the jobs? Or is it just another layer of the same old game, where loyalty is as flexible as rubber?

Now, flow into this one about the medics who help keep us breathing – those resident doctors who've been downing tools, striking for better pay and conditions, and our authorities are scrambling to wrap it up. They announce moves to end it, meetings upon meetings, promises flying left and right. It's touching, really, because when push comes to shove, health isn't something you joke with. But let's be real, how many times have we heard "soon" turn into "maybe next year"? That's our reality – faith stepping in where systems falter. And it's true, even in the chaos, there's that quiet strength, like the Bible says in James, pure religion is caring for the sick and the poor. So while the talks drag on, we're holding onto hope, because if doctors can't strike gold, at least grace covers the gaps.

Ah, but don't get me started on these global handshakes our top man is doing, like that powwow with the World Bank folks right here at home. They're jetting in, suits crisp, briefcases full of figures, discussing loans and reforms as if it's a business deal over pounded yam. On one hand, it's promising – fresh funds to tackle the jobless queues that stretch longer than Third Mainland on a Monday morning. On the other, you wonder if it'll trickle down or just fatten the elite's pockets. They talk big about sustainable growth, anti-corruption drives, but we've heard tunes like this before, remixed with local flavor. Imagine the boardroom: charts showing unemployment stats climbing like prices at Jankara market, and the bankers nodding sagely, suggesting more taxes or cuts. Our side pushes back, saying our pace is fine, no rush to fix everything overnight. Fair point, because rushing in this heat? You'd sweat out all your sense. Yet, for the youth hustling with CVs in hand, it's frustrating – dreaming of that steady gig while dodging 419 calls promising quick riches. Self-deprecating as it sounds, we Nigerians turn it into art: one boy I know started a side hustle selling phone credits during blackouts, turning "no light" into steady cash. That's resilience, the kind that Proverbs celebrates – diligence leading to wealth, even if it's kobo by kobo.

And tying it all, there's that birthday nod to the old soldier who ruled back in the day, now hitting eighty-four. Roads half-built, memories of decrees, and lessons in survival. IBB, with his gap-toothed smile, reminds us that time flies, and even the tough ones mellow. Makes you reflect, shey? In a land where yesterday's foe is today's ally, it's all about grace – forgiving the past, hoping for better. Like Paul in the epistles, pressing toward the mark, leaving the old behind. So while politicians dance their tango, we the people keep faith, because in the end, it's not the threats or the switches that define us, but how we rise, laughing through the storm.
