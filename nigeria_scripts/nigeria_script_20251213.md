Now, shifting gears to our own backyard drama, where loyalty in politics feels like yesterday's akara—hot one minute, forgotten the next. Take this Bayelsa chief executive, the one who was PDP poster boy, suddenly flipping the script to pitch tent with APC. But seriously, if switching sides na the new normal, make we all start practicing—today Labour, tomorrow SDP, by weekend na fresh party wey I go form myself. Call am Eze Party, where promises no dey expire like garri for rain season.

Speaking of promises that stretch like elastic band, let's talk this matter of our medical warriors, the resident doctors wey don tire of waiting for better days. Strike again, hospitals quiet like Sunday morning church before sermon. Government says dem dey move to end it, but how many times we hear that chorus? It's like that stubborn uncle who promises to send money every Christmas but shows up empty-handed with excuses taller than Osun bridge. These doctors, God bless their stethoscopes, dey fight for salaries wey fit buy proper shoes, not the ones wey dey leak during harmattan. Imagine patient dey bed, doctor on picket line chanting for better pay—na who go attend to malaria wey no dey take holiday? Me, I dey pray say this one end quick, because even Jesus wey heal without charge no strike, but if na today, e for don form union against Pharisees wey dey chop all the temple money. Grace go sort am, sha; one day, our health system go shine like promised land, no more queue for paracetamol like it's Black Friday sale.

President sends warm words, congratulating the man who once held the baton with iron grip. E be like family reunion where old stories dey flow, but nobody wan remember the dark chapters. Nigerians reading this, una dey smile or dey shake head? How some reach that age with medals and memoirs, others still hustling for daily bread. Yet, in all this, there's that quiet hope, like Proverbs say, that wisdom comes with gray hairs, not just gray pockets. So, happy birthday, General; may your years ahead bring peace, not more coups in disguise.

Brothers, e sweet to hear, but reality check: while he's wooing dollars, back home, danfo drivers dey dodge water-logged roads like obstacle course. Investors listening, make una come with life jackets, because Lagos rain no dey play. And climate change? Na the one wey make NEPA dance skelewu every evening, lights off before you spell "sustainability." But credit to the man; at least e dey try to globalize our hustle. Imagine if every pothole na pitch for foreign cash— we'd be swimming in euros by now. Yet, in the mix of it, there's that faith nudge: even as waters rise, like Noah's ark, we go float through by divine design, no more sinking ships in traffic jam.

But that's us—resilient, laughing through the storm, turning sorrow to satire. And in the heart of it, remember, as the Good Book whispers, trials build character, not break spirit.
