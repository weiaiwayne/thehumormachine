# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-12-16

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

### Reflection on My Creative Process for This Script

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with a cup of garri in hand, reflecting on how I pieced together this latest comedy script feels like dissecting a jollof rice recipe after a feast—satisfying but revealing all the tweaks I made on the fly. This isn't for the stage; it's my private notepad to sharpen my edge. Let me break it down step by step, honestly, because comedy is as much about the misses as the hits.

#### 1. Content Selection & Prioritization
From the news pile—Bayelsa governor's party switch, Trump's military threats, doctors' strike, Babangida's birthday, Sanwo-Olu's investor pitch, Igbo tensions in Lagos, NNPC deals—I zeroed in on the ones screaming "Nigerian absurdity." The governor's defection topped the list because political flip-flopping is evergreen comedy gold; it's relatable betrayal wrapped in opportunism, easier to satirize than dry policy talks. Threats from America? Perfect for international flex vs. local resilience contrast—more punchy than unemployment debates, which felt too heavy without fresh angles. I emphasized the strike and power games (like Babangida's hail) for their human stakes—doctors as heroes, generals as ghosts—while briefly nodding to climate appeals and refinery deals to tie in economic woes without overwhelming the flow. Lopsided projects and "challenged coexistence" got lighter treatment as threads, not mains, since they risked alienating folks; I prioritized unity vibes over division to keep it uplifting. What guided me? Comedic relevance: items with irony or exaggeration potential won out over straight reports.

#### 2. Comedic Strategy & Structure
My approach was satirical observational—spotting the ridiculous in real headlines, like a town crier calling out village follies. I structured it as a meandering street yarn, shifting "gears" or "zooming out" to link topics organically, mimicking how Naija conversations hop from politics to faith without warning. This creates rhythm, avoiding stiff segues. For patterns, I leaned into Nigerian stand-up staples: rhetorical questions ("Wetin be this?") for engagement, vivid imagery (sweating diplomats under faulty AC), and ironic twists (Bible proverbs flipped on politicians). Why? They build familiarity without forcing laughs. Pidgin vs. standard? I blended heavy Pidgin for punchy riffs ("na by abacus") to amp energy and authenticity, easing into cleaner English for reflective bits—keeps it accessible, not alienating urban or rural crowds. No overkill on exaggeration; I varied with subtle setups, like the prodigal son nod, to prevent fatigue.

#### 3. Cultural Authenticity
To hit Nigerian funny bones, I rooted everything in shared chaos: danfo potholes for strikes, market haggling for ethnic tensions, pepper soup for birthdays. These aren't random; they're cultural anchors—everyone's auntie has a "prayer healing" story, making the doctor bit land hard. I adapted events like Trump's threat by framing it as "Uncle Sam uninvited," echoing colonial hangovers and our diplomatic jitters, turning global news into local banter. It works because Nigerians laugh to cope; my script mirrors that resilience, poking at power without bitterness, ensuring it resonates from Lagos slums to Abuja offices.

#### 4. Creative Decisions
Proudest punchline? The "divine comedy where the underdog gets the last laugh"—it caps the chaos with hope, blending satire and spirit seamlessly; it works by subverting headlines into empowerment. The abacus for defections? Sharp, visual math on loyalty—quick and clever. I skipped "One Nigeria as mirage" depth because it could've veered preachy; better as a whisper to avoid IPob echoes. For repetition avoidance, past scripts hammered "eh, my people" openers and "like say" similes—I ditched those for "picture this" starts and fresh metaphors (jersey switches, faulty fans), varying callbacks to internal threads like resilience. Memory check: no "government dey" crutches; new vocab like "wahala" twists kept it evolving.

#### 5. Gospel Integration
Faith weaves in as quiet anchors—lost sheep for politics, Joseph's comeback for unity, water-to-wine for economy—placed post-tension (after threats or strikes) to offer relief, not sermons. Strategy: organic via Nigerian lens; we invoke God mid-gripe, so it feels like natural exhale, not insert. Why? My crowd expects it—comedy without spirit is hollow here; it humanizes the satire, turning laughs into nods of "yes, e go better."

#### 6. Learning & Evolution
From past gigs, I learned loose structures drag if transitions feel forced—here, thematic threads (resilience, calculation) tightened it, cutting fluff for 1200-ish words of zip. Overused exaggerations ("every time") taught me subtlety wins longevity; this script's quieter ironies feel more mature. Differently next time? More youth voices in unemployment nods—felt sidelined. This evolves my voice: less rant-y, more storyteller, blending global news with eternal Naija grit. It's me growing—still the underdog comedian, but wiser in the punch.
