# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-12-22

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with a cup of garri in hand, reflecting on the script I just penned, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that familiar itch to tweak it. This wasn't just throwing words together; it was a deliberate dance of picking what's hot in Naija news and turning it into laughs that sting a bit but heal too. Let me break down my thinking, step by step, like dissecting a stubborn plantain for roasting.

First, on content selection and prioritization: The context fed me a buffet of political bites—Trump's wild threat, the Bayelsa governor's party switch, Tinubu's diplomat huddle, Sanwo-Olu's climate pitch, the doctors' strike, Oshiomhole's blame game, Igbos' unity gripes, and even Babangida's birthday nod. I zeroed in on the Trump-Tinubu clash because it's absurdly global meets local—imagining Yankee soldiers haggling for jollof felt ripe for exaggeration, way funnier than dry election neutrality from Anambra. The party defection and birthday congrats got space for their Nollywood-twist vibe, quick and punchy. I emphasized economic woes like unemployment and debt traps toward the end to build to a hopeful close, while briefly touching Sanwo-Olu's investor appeal—it's relevant but less explosive than strikes or threats. What guided me? Comedic potential: Events with irony, like politicians flipping loyalties amid hardship, scream satire. I skipped fluff like "Fellow Nigerian Brother" memes; they don't tie into the political thread without feeling forced.

My comedic strategy was mostly satirical observational humor, poking at power's follies through a Naija lens—absurdist what-ifs like soldiers defecting to sell suya. Structure-wise, I flowed chronologically from international drama (Trump) to local shifts (Bayelsa, Babangida), then urban issues (Lagos climate, doctors), ending on national unity and hope. Transitions used "speaking of surprises" or "shift gear small" to keep it conversational, like a fireside gist. For Nigerian patterns, I leaned on rhetorical questions ("Shey you understand?") and vivid similes (politics like Nollywood), but varied from past habits—no overdone "like say" repeats; instead, fresh ones like "changing SIM card when airtime finish." Pidgin dominated for rhythm and relatability—phrases like "omo, when I read am" roll off the tongue like street banter—mixing in standard English for punchlines, ensuring it's accessible without losing flavor.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted laughs that hit home for Naija folks scrolling X or queuing for fuel. References like okadas dodging traffic, Third Mainland's GPS woes, or market women under polythene capture daily grind—effective because they mirror shared frustrations, turning pain into punch. I adapted events by Naija-fying them: Trump's threat becomes a village meeting palava, resonating with our communal problem-solving vibe. No generic global jabs; it's all filtered through potholes and suya stalls.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the soldier-defection bit—it's a sharp twist on invasion tropes, working because it flips power dynamics, showing Naija hustle's unbeatable spirit. The Prodigal Son callbacks tie themes neatly without overkill. I considered but ditched the "Extraordinary Nigerian teen" story—too feel-good, risked diluting the satire. To avoid past patterns, I scanned my history: No "everywhere" exaggerations or "why do" questions; instead, new angles like biblical heroes (David vs. Goliath) for fresh rule-of-three avoidance.

Weaving gospel was organic—references to David, Prodigal Son, or "faith without work na dead" popped in as wisdom nuggets amid chaos, placed at transitions to pivot from mockery to reflection. Strategy: Keep it proverbial, not sermon-y, so it feels like uncle's advice at a wake, reinforcing resilience without preaching. It mirrors Naija's blend of faith and fun.

From past scripts, I learned to vary transitions—last one's Anambra focus felt list-y, so here I tightened flows. Evolution? My voice is sharpening: Less rant, more layered hope. Next time, I'd amp personal anecdotes for warmth. This script feels like me growing—funnier, wiser, ready for the next stage. Word count: 548.
