# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2026-01-01

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notepad after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that nagging itch to dissect what just poured out of me. Writing comedy as a Nigerian isn't just about laughs—it's therapy for the soul, turning our daily madness into something we can chuckle at while nodding to the divine hand steering it all. Let me break down my process step by step, like peeling yam for pounding.

First, on content selection and prioritization: From the context pile—those headlines about Trump's bluster, the Bayelsa governor's party hop, Babangida's milestone, doctors striking, the NNPC-Dangote naira deal, Sanwo-Olu's climate pitch, and whispers of lopsided projects—I zeroed in on the ones screaming "absurdity" for comedy. Trump's threat stood out because it's international drama clashing with our local vibe; it's comedically relevant as it highlights Nigeria's diplomatic dance, which feels like a Nollywood plot twist. The governor's defection and APC's praise for Tinubu's neutrality? Pure gold for satire on political flip-flopping—it's timely and mirrors our fluid alliances. I emphasized these heavily because they tie into power games, which Nigerians love to roast. Briefly mentioned unemployment and Igbos' unity gripes to weave in broader tensions without dominating, as the context had echoes of past scripts (like job hunts), so I kept them transitional. I skipped deeper dives into things like Tinubu's Japan trip or IMF replies—too policy-wonky, less punchy for laughs, and risked overlapping with my recent travel yarns.

My comedic strategy was observational satire laced with absurdity, poking at the ridiculousness of Nigerian life without outright bitterness. I structured it as a yarn session: starting with global threats to hook energy, flowing to local switches and honors for emotional rhythm, then health/economy for relatability, ending on unity and faith for uplift. Transitions like "That brings me to" or "Shifting gears" created a conversational chain, like chatting at a beer parlor. For Nigerian patterns, I leaned on vivid similes (market women haggling, okada changing lanes) because they ground the satire in everyday chaos—effective for evoking "ah, true talk!" moments. I balanced Pidgin heavily (80%) for that raw, street-smart flavor—phrases like "chai, the confusion" feel authentic—while slipping in standard English for punchlines, ensuring it's accessible without losing edge.

Cultural authenticity was key; I aimed for resonance by embedding references like harmattan coughs, immigration queues, danfo madness, and generator noise—these aren't just jokes; they're shared scars that make audiences lean in, feeling seen. Adapting events? Trump's troops imagine our bureaucracy as the real weapon—it's a Nigerian twist on foreign invasion, turning fear into farce, which fits our resilient humor.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the Babangida punchline: his imagined quip about coup-ing high blood pressure. It works because it humanizes a legend, blending respect with irreverence, landing a gentle twist on history. The doctors' strike bit—politicians calling pastors for colds—nails the irony of elite hypocrisy while nodding to faith. I considered lopsided projects more but pulled back; it risked sounding accusatory, and with Igbos' claims, I didn't want to fuel division—comedy should unite, not divide. To avoid past patterns, I ditched overused exaggerations like "everywhere" or callbacks like "speaking of"; instead, fresh setups like "imagine if" for hypotheticals, and new phrases—"quick quick," "turn back sharp sharp"—kept it vibrant, not recycled.

For gospel integration, I wove in faith organically as anchors: Bible verses on healing after doctors, Psalms' light post-blackout, Christ's body for unity. Strategy? Place them as hopeful codas to sections, not sermons— they emerge from the chaos naturally, like prayers in a storm, reminding folks God's got the last laugh without preaching. It softens the satire, aligning with my voice as a believer-comedian.

From past scripts, I learned to vary flows—my December ones were jet-set heavy, so this grounded in homefront drama. Evolution? I'm honing a warmer tone; less chaotic rants, more reflective yarns, building my voice as the wise-cracking uncle who laughs through tears. Next time, I'd tighten transitions for snappier pace—some sections lingered. This script feels like growth: funnier, faith-fuller, and fiercely Nigerian. By God's grace, I'll keep evolving.
