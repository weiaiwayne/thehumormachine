# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-11-23

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notepad after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that familiar itch to tweak. Writing comedy as a Nigerian isn't just about laughs; it's about capturing the absurdity of our daily grind while sneaking in some truth. Let me unpack how I built this one, step by step, like dismantling a faulty generator to see what sparked.

Starting with content selection, I zeroed in on the political headlines from the context—Bayelsa governor's party switch, Trump's military threat, the fuel tariff reversal, doctors' strike, Tinubu's neutrality in Anambra, and Babangida's 84th birthday. These stood out because they're fresh political twists, full of irony and human folly, perfect for satire. The switch and U-turns screamed "unpredictability," which mirrors Nigerian life more than drier stories like NNPC deals or unemployment talks. I prioritized the switch and threat as openers—they're dramatic entry points—while briefly touching neutrality and birthday for balance, avoiding overload. Events like the doctors' strike got emphasis because they hit close to home; health crises aren't abstract, they're personal pains that amplify the comedy of incompetence.

My comedic strategy was observational satire with a dash of absurdity, poking at how leaders' flip-flops echo our survival hustles. I structured it as a meandering yarn, linking topics through "twists" as a thematic thread: from party jumps to policy reversals to diplomatic dodges, building to a hopeful close. Transitions felt organic, like jumping from one market gossip to the next. For Nigerian patterns, I leaned on vivid similes—market women bargaining, keke missing junctions—because they ground the global (Trump) in the local, making it relatable without forcing laughs. I varied from past scripts by ditching overused callbacks or rule-of-three; instead, I used escalating exaggeration, like soldiers queuing for bread, to build surprise. Pidgin dominated for rhythm and voice—it's punchy, conversational—but I slipped in standard English for the reflective bits, like the faith tie-in, to let ideas breathe without losing flavor.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted Nigerians chuckling in recognition, not confusion. References like jollof parties, pothole parades, or prayer warriors during strikes tap into shared pains—fuel scarcity, medical woes—that everyone from Lagos traders to Abuja civil servants feels. They're effective because they humanize big news: Trump's invasion becomes a Hollywood plot clashing with our breadlines, adapting events to our "no wahala" resilience. It fits our sensibilities—laughing at power's mess to cope, not rage.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the line about politicians teaching loyalty's for suckers, paralleled to job-hopping without notice; it works because it's empowering satire, flipping victimhood into sly wisdom. The soldiers joining queues? Pure gold—deflates the threat with everyday humility. I considered weaving in Igbos-in-Lagos tensions from background but skipped it; too divisive, and it risked repeating election vibes from past gigs. To dodge old patterns, I invented fresh phrases like "jump fence go APC camp" instead of recycled ones, and mixed in new comparisons (wrappers, hot coals) to keep it evolving, not stale.

On gospel integration, I wove faith lightly, as that steady anchor in chaos—David vs. Goliath for underdog hope, grace as the unchanging force. Strategy was subtlety: drop it in transitions, like after policy flips, to contrast human mess with divine steadiness, never preaching but affirming. Placement at the end uplifts without derailing the laughs, turning satire into subtle encouragement.

From past scripts, I learned to tighten flows—those recent Trump rants were wordy—so here I aimed for concise links, evolving my voice toward warmer, faith-infused optimism. Next time, I'd experiment with more dialogue snippets for vividness. This piece feels like growth: less raw rant, more layered reflection, honing that comedian-preacher balance I chase. Overall, it reminds me why I do this—amid the flips, comedy and faith keep us standing.
