# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-11-27

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here in my Lagos flat with a lukewarm bottle of Star beer and my notebook scribbled full of half-formed ideas, I figure it's time to unpack this script I just banged out. Writing comedy about Naija's endless drama isn't just about laughs—it's like wrestling a stubborn goat; you gotta know why it bucks the way it does. This reflection is for me, to sharpen my edge before the next mic drop. Let's dissect it properly.

Starting with content selection and prioritization: From the pile of headlines—Trump's bluster, Bayelsa governor's flip-flop, Babangida's birthday, oil policy U-turns, World Bank chats on jobs, and that Igbo-Lagos tension—I zeroed in on the political heavy-hitters because they're ripe for satire. The diplomat summons screamed absurdity, like a village council calling in the world, so I led with it for a strong hook. The defection and birthday got prime slots too; defections mirror our fluid loyalties, and honoring elders at 84? That's gold for generational jabs. I skimmed lighter stuff like the Nigerian drummer's confessions or food rankings—fun, but not punchy enough for the script's flow. Unemployment and favoritism got briefer nods to tie into the economic woes, emphasizing survival over deep dives, 'cause I wanted breadth, not a lecture. Prioritized what felt universally frustrating yet comical to everyday folks, avoiding niche bits that might dilute the energy.

My comedic strategy was observational satire laced with absurdity—watching Naija politics like a Nollywood farce, exaggerating the chaos to highlight resilience. Structure-wise, I flowed from global threats (diplomacy) inward to local gripes (creeks, Lagos bias), building to a hopeful close on our unshakeable spirit. Transitions were organic, like linking oil wahala to broader economy via "black gold," keeping it conversational, not choppy. I drew on Nigerian patterns like everyday analogies—phone credit for loyalty, okada speed for threats—'cause they ground the satire in familiarity, making the ridiculous feel real. Pidgin dominated for rhythm and authenticity (that "na wa oh" vibe), but I sprinkled standard English in reflective bits to ease pacing and appeal beyond the streets. Why? Pure balance—too much Pidgin risks alienating, too little loses the flavor.

For cultural authenticity, I leaned hard into what makes Naija humor tick: shared pains and joys. References like kola nut at palavers, harmattan weather flips, or pothole parties scream local life, evoking that "ehn, true talk" nod from audiences. Adapting events? Trump's threat became a blockbuster movie standoff, fitting our love for dramatic spins on serious ish. It resonates 'cause we laugh to cope—turning IMF timelines into NEPA unreliability captures that collective eye-roll at empty promises.

Creative decisions: I'm chuffed with punchlines like diplomacy as a "family meeting with kola nut" or defections expiring "like phone credit"—they work 'cause they're vivid, tying big news to pocket-sized truths, landing with a wink. The oil U-turn as a "danfo dodging LASTMA" had me chuckling while writing; it's visual, chaotic, pure Lagos. I toyed with the Japan trip but ditched it—too travelogue, not enough bite amid hotter scandals. Past performances warned me off repeats: no overdoing "oga at the top" or Lagos rants, so I freshened language with "head honcho" and new angles like biblical heroes in politics. Varied patterns too—swapped rule-of-three for solo setups, avoided "but wait" callbacks for seamless segues.

On gospel integration: Faith's my anchor, so I wove it organically as hope's thread—David vs. Goliath for diplomacy grit, Paul for defection redemption, Job for oil losses. Strategy? Drop them at section tails, like grace notes, to uplift without sermonizing; it's comedy with soul, not altar call. Placed there 'cause punchlines need a soft landing—turns satire sweet, reminding us God's in the mix amid the madness.

From past scripts, I learned to connect dots tighter—those old ones jumped too wildly, so this flows like a gist session. Evolution? My voice is maturing: less raw Pidgin rants, more layered faith ties and subtle absurdity. Next time, I'd add personal stories for warmth, maybe shorten transitions to amp pace. This one's sharper, more resilient—like Naija itself. Onward.
