# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-12-05

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notebook after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that nagging itch to dissect what just poured out of my head. Writing comedy as a Nigerian isn't just about laughs; it's therapy for the soul in this madhouse we call home. Let me break down how and why I shaped this piece the way I did—raw thoughts, no filter.

Starting with content selection and prioritization, I had a buffet of news from the context: Trump threats, party switches, tariffs, strikes, refinery deals, and even that teen story. I zeroed in on the political absurdities because they're ripe for satire—things like the Bayelsa governor's defection and the fuel tariff U-turn scream "Naija wahala" in neon lights. These felt more comedically potent than, say, the World Bank meeting or unemployment talks, which overlap too much with my past gigs on government and economy. I emphasized the interconnected political drama (Trump to diplomats, neutrality in Anambra, Babangida's birthday) for a unified narrative arc, treating them as a chain of escalating ridiculousness. Briefer mentions, like the NNPC deal, served as palate cleansers—tying back to economic relief without dominating. I skipped the "extraordinary teen" entirely; it's feel-good fluff that could've diluted the edge, and the Igbos in Lagos or lopsided projects felt too divisive for humor right now.

My comedic strategy was observational satire with a dash of absurdity, poking at the flip-flops and hypocrisies in Nigerian politics like a mirror to our daily grind. Structure-wise, I flowed chronologically from international threats to local maneuvers, using transitions like "speaking of switching sides" to mimic how real-life news bleeds into one another—keeps the energy bouncing like a danfo on potholes. I leaned on Nigerian patterns like rhetorical questions ("Shey e no go better?") and vivid analogies (politicians switching like chameleons), because they ground the satire in relatability without forcing setup-punchlines, which I've overused before. Pidgin dominated for rhythm and voice—raw, street-level English for punchy lines, blending into standard for reflective bits to avoid alienating broader listeners. It's 70-30 Pidgin, enough to feel authentic without turning it into a lecture.

For cultural authenticity, I aimed straight at the Nigerian pulse: references to queuing for ATMs, neutral potholes swallowing danfos, or mixing faith with frenzy hit home because they're universal pains here. Jollof as a party staple or "no gree for anybody" as an anthem? Those are effective because they evoke shared nostalgia and resilience—Nigerians laugh hardest at our own survival hacks. I adapted events like Trump's threat by framing it through our history of threats (colonial to civil war), turning global bluster into local defiance, which fits our comedic sensibility of underdog triumph laced with irony.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the chameleon switch for the governor and the "neutral pothole" jab at Tinubu's Anambra stance—they work because they're visual, tying elite politics to everyday suffering without meanness. The prayer in traffic jam callback? Gold, as it flips frustration into wit. I considered the doctors' strike in depth but trimmed it to avoid overlapping my old fuel and health rants; it could've felt repetitive. To dodge past patterns, I skipped "like say" overload and rule-of-three lists, opting for fluid exaggerations and fresh phrases like "policy flip-flop na wetin dem call am for book" to keep it evolving.

On gospel integration, I wove faith organically as the script's emotional spine—phrases like "God's grace sufficient" or turning "scarcity to abundance" emerge in moments of reflection, like after the tariff flip, to underscore hope amid chaos. Strategy was subtlety: no sermons, just cultural nods to how we Nigerians blend church with life (NEPA outage in prayer meeting), placed at pivots to lift from cynicism to resilience without preachiness. It mirrors my belief that comedy heals when it points upward.

From past scripts, I learned to vary flows—my last one was too callback-heavy, so I focused on seamless segues here. This evolved my voice: less rant-y, more hopeful satire, blending the "Nigerian condition" with unshakeable spirit. Next time, I'd tighten transitions for punchier pacing; some sections dragged. Overall, this script feels like growth—laughing through the fire, one honest yarn at a time.
