# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-12-31

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here in my small Lagos flat with a cup of Milo cooling on the table, I decide to pull back the curtain on how I crafted that latest script. It's not every day I analyze my own brain like this, but after reading it over, I see the threads of my process clearer. This reflection is just for me, a way to sharpen my edge before the next performance. Let's break it down step by step.

First, on content selection and prioritization: I had a pile of news items from the context—politics, threats, investments, strikes, and more. I zeroed in on the Bayelsa governor's party switch, Tinubu's birthday wishes to Babangida, Trump's military threats, Sanwo-Olu's climate appeal, unemployment talks, the NNPC refinery deal, Igbo-Lagos tensions, and the World Bank meeting. Why these? They screamed irony and absurdity, perfect for comedy—politicians flipping loyalties like market traders, old generals getting cakes, and foreign threats while we battle potholes. I prioritized the party jump and threats because they felt like fresh Nollywood plot twists, tying into everyday survival. Unemployment and the refinery got more emphasis as they hit the "small man" like me directly; I mentioned strikes and coexistence briefly to connect dots without overwhelming the flow. I skipped things like Anambra elections or Japan trips because my memory warned against repeating election or travel beats from past scripts—too close to abuja, election, or president vibes.

For comedic strategy and structure, I went for a satirical observational style, blending everyday Nigerian frustration with absurd exaggerations to poke at power without outright bitterness. The structure flowed like a market yarn: starting with local politics, layering international drama, shifting to economic woes, and ending on unity and hope. I transitioned with phrases like "that one alone no finish" or "shift gear small" to mimic casual gist, keeping it rhythmic. Nigerian patterns? I leaned on irony (military rulers now celebrated) and wordplay (gates locked, cake with poison), avoiding overused callbacks or rule-of-three from my history. Pidgin dominated for punchy rhythm—"wetin be this na?" feels alive, urgent—while standard English crept in for reflective bits, balancing accessibility for wider audiences without losing street cred.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted laughs that echo in a danfo or beer parlor. References like danfo buses, akara, jollof, okada exhaust as "pepper soup," and market haggling ground it in Naija life—these aren't random; they're shared pains and joys that make the satire bite. Adapting events, I twisted Trump's threat into "pay up or boom" versus our road "war zones," fitting our sensibilities where global noise amplifies local chaos, turning fear into relatable mockery.

Creative decisions: I'm proudest of the fish seller joke—"Trump threaten, so fish dey fear, price climb"—it works because it's a punchy, visual escalation linking high politics to low-stakes daily grind, sparking that "e true o" chuckle. The ant-for-sugar resilience line ties humor to hope without forcing it. I considered lopsided projects but nixed it; it risked deepening division, and with Igbo-Lagos already in, it felt redundant. To dodge past patterns, I invented new phrasing—no "dey" overload, no "like say" repeats—instead, fresh twists like "chop jollof and go" or "concrete jungle wey need God's rain."

Gospel integration happened organically, my secret sauce as a faith-driven comedian. I wove in Bible nods—peace surpassing understanding, waiting on the Lord, Proverbs on building—like proverbs in conversation, not sermons. Placed them at tension peaks (threats, unemployment) for relief, ending with Jesus as the steady redeemer to uplift without preaching. Strategy: make faith the quiet anchor, showing resilience through it, so it feels like part of the Nigerian spirit, not tacked on.

From past scripts, I learned to vary energy—earlier ones were jet-off heavy; this one's more grounded in connections. I evolved by tightening transitions for better pace, avoiding exaggeration fatigue. Next time, I'd experiment more with self-deprecating bits to humanize further. Overall, this script honed my voice: satirical yet hopeful, Pidgin-powered, faith-infused. It's me growing, turning news chaos into communal laughter. Word count: 548.
