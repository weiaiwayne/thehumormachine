# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-12-20

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with a cup of hot pap and some chin-chin on the side, I step back to unpack how I crafted this latest script. It's like peeling back the layers of a plantain to see what makes the sweet part tick. This reflection is just for me, a way to sharpen my craft without the spotlight.

First, on content selection and prioritization: I sifted through the context like a market woman sorting tomatoes—picking the ripest for the pot. The core political developments screamed for attention: Tinubu's neutrality in Anambra, the Trump threat, Bayelsa governor's switch, Babangida's birthday, doctors' strike, Sanwo-Olu's climate appeal, and the NNPC-Dangote deal. These felt fresh and urgent, tying into Nigeria's chaotic political pulse, which is comedy gold. I prioritized the Trump saga because it's absurdly international, easy to exaggerate for laughs, and contrasts hilariously with our diplomatic jabs. The party switch and refinery deal got emphasis for their local intrigue—switching parties is a Naija staple, and the naira-for-crude angle pokes at economic woes without repeating fuel subsidy rants from past gigs. Lighter bits like Babangida's birthday and the strike were mentioned briefly to add warmth and relatability, avoiding overload. I skipped background noise like unemployment talks or Igbos in Lagos; they felt too divisive or stale, not punchy enough for satire.

My comedic strategy was mainly satirical observational humor, poking fun at the ridiculousness of power plays while keeping an undercurrent of hope. I structured it as a flowing monologue, like a pidgin yarn around a bonfire—starting with Tinubu's neutrality as an opener to set the political tone, then weaving through Trump, party jumps, birthday, strike, climate, and refinery, ending with a unifying twist on faith and laughter. Transitions were organic, using "speaking on that note" or "but let me yarn" to mimic street-side gist, building momentum without abrupt jumps. I drew on Nigerian patterns like hyperbole (politics as "chess with football boots") and rhetorical questions ("which kain neutrality be that?") because they amplify our everyday exasperation, making the audience nod and chuckle. Pidgin dominated—about 80%—for that raw, accessible vibe, dipping into standard English for punchlines or emphasis, like in the Nollywood reference, to vary rhythm and avoid monotony.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted it to feel like homegrown ribbing, not outsider mockery. References to jollof, traffic jams, market bargaining, and Nollywood villains ground it in Naija life—these are shared touchstones that evoke "ah, that's us!" without explanation. Adapting events, I twisted Trump's threat into a bully-backyard joke, fitting our resilience narrative, and the refinery deal became a family potluck analogy, resonating with communal economics. It's effective because it mirrors how we Nigerians laugh at pain to survive, turning global slights into local triumphs.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the Trump-to-investment flip: "maybe Trump go wake up tomorrow and say, ah Nigeria, una people too sharp, make I send investment instead of invasion." It works for the absurd optimism, subverting fear into fantasy partnership, a classic Naija cope mechanism. The doctors' strike punchline—"healing still come from above, whether from doctor or divine touch"—ties humor to heart seamlessly. I considered the World Bank meeting but ditched it; too dry, risked overlapping with economic repeats from memory. To dodge past patterns, I avoided overused exaggerations like "everywhere confusion" or callbacks like "speaking of," opting for fresh similes (e.g., "villain go turn friend") and new phrases like "goals dey fly left and right" instead of "dem dey."

Gospel integration was subtle, my signature quiet faith thread. I wove in biblical nods—like long life as blessing for Babangida or grace sustaining strikes—organically, as reflective asides rather than sermons, placed mid-topic to humanize the satire. Strategy: Use them as emotional anchors, showing politicians' promises pale against divine assurance, ending with "true peace come from higher place." It feels natural because faith is woven into Naija discourse; no preachiness, just honest hope to uplift without alienating.

From past scripts, I learned to vary flow—last ones started too punchy, so this builds gradually. Evolution-wise, my voice is maturing: less rant, more layered wit, blending satire with subtle inspiration. Next time, I'd tighten transitions for shorter airtime and experiment with more visual gags in delivery. This script feels like growth—fresher, faith-infused, and fiercely Naija. Time to brew the next one.
