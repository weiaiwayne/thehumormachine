# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2026-01-16

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notebook after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that familiar itch to dissect what just poured out of me. Writing comedy isn't just about laughs; it's about channeling the chaos of Naija life into something that bites without breaking. Let me break down my process step by step, like peeling yam for pounding.

First, on content selection and prioritization: The context threw a buffet of news at me—Trump's threats, the Bayelsa governor's party switch, Babangida's birthday, doctors' strike, Sanwo-Olu's climate pitch, World Bank talks, Igbo-Lagos tensions, and Tinubu's trips. I zeroed in on the ones with high dramatic potential for satire, like the diplomat summons and party defection, because they scream absurdity in Nigerian politics—leaders reacting faster to foreign bluster than local crises. I emphasized these for their timeliness and emotional pull; Trump's threat feels like an external bully picking on a family squabble, perfect for exaggeration. Briefer mentions went to things like the Japan-Brazil trip or unemployment, as they served as transitions without stealing the spotlight. I skipped fluff like "ranking Nigerian foods" or "Confessions of a Nigerian Drummer" because they lacked the political edge to tie into the script's unity theme. What made them comedically relevant? They mirror the Nigerian condition—promises vs. reality—allowing me to layer irony without forcing connections.

My comedic strategy was primarily satirical observational humor, poking at power structures with a lens of everyday frustration, blended with light absurdism (like switching parties as "changing churches for miracles"). I structured the flow chronologically and thematically: starting with international threats to hook with urgency, pivoting to domestic politics (Bayelsa, Babangida), then social issues (strikes, climate), and ending on broader hopes (economy, unity, trips). This creates a narrative arc from chaos to cautious optimism, like a Nollywood film building to redemption. For Nigerian patterns, I leaned into vivid metaphors (politics as market bargaining) and rhetorical questions ("Wahala for who?"), which feel like street banter—engaging, not scripted. I balanced Pidgin (for rhythm and relatability) with standard English (for punchy asides), keeping Pidgin dominant in dialogue-like sections to mimic oral storytelling, while English clarified faith ties.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted it to hit home for Naija folks scrolling Punch or Guardian. References like "emergency jollof rice distribution" or "paddling canoe to market" draw from shared experiences—floods in Bayelsa, unreliable AC in Abuja meetings—making the humor intimate, not distant. Adapting events? I twisted Sanwo-Olu's investor appeal into roasting Lagos dangers (okadas, potholes), fitting our sensibilities of resilience amid mess, turning global news into local griping.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the party-switch joke: "why we no fit switch like that for everyday life? Imagine dumping your bank loan." It works because it's relatable hypocrisy—politicians' fluidity vs. our stuckness—building empathy before the laugh. I considered but ditched the "Extraordinary Nigerian teen" story; it felt too inspirational for satire, risking sentimentality. To avoid past patterns, I skipped overused exaggerations like "everywhere" or callbacks like "speaking of," opting for fresh setups (e.g., faith as "higher power wey heals without bill") and new phrases like "scatter am" instead of "dem dey." No repeats of old topics like fuel or elections directly; I angled fresh on diplomacy and climate.

For gospel integration, I wove it organically as reflective breathers—after strikes, a nod to "grace wey covers every shortfall," or post-division, the prodigal son for reconciliation. Strategy: Tie it to punchlines, not sermons, so it feels like wisdom from an uncle, not preaching. Placement at section ends builds uplift, countering satire's cynicism without preaching.

From past scripts, I learned to vary transitions—those old ones dragged with "but" setups—so here I used "shift gear" for energy. This evolved my voice: less rant-y, more layered with hope, showing growth from pure mockery to nuanced commentary. Next time, I'd tighten transitions for pace; some felt wordy. Overall, this script feels like me sharpening my tool—laughs that heal a bit.
