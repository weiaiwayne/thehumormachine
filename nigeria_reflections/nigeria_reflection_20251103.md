# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-11-03

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notebook after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that familiar itch to tweak it further. Writing comedy isn't just about the laughs; it's about dissecting why the jokes land or flop in my head first. Let me break down my process for this one—it's like peeling plantain for dodo, layer by layer.

Starting with content selection and prioritization, I had a buffet of news from the context: Tinubu's Trump meetup, hunger docs up north, that $8bn energy windfall, refinery talks, Christian aid queries, Igbos-in-Lagos debates, and even drummer confessions. I zeroed in on the big political and economic bites because they're ripe for satire—they mirror the absurdity of Nigerian life without needing much setup. The Trump-Tinubu handshake? Perfect opener; it's timely and lets me riff on global posturing versus local ghost towns. Hunger and energy investments got more airtime because they hit the gut—everyone feels empty pockets or blackouts. I breezed past fluff like food rankings or the PDP convention; the former's too light, and the latter smelled like old territory from my past gigs. Prioritization came from relevance: I wanted topics that chain together, showing interconnected woes, not a disjointed list. Comedically, the juicier ones (like naira-for-crude deals) screamed exaggeration potential, while drier ones like coexistence challenges got a nod for closure without dominating.

My comedic strategy was observational satire with a dash of absurdity—poking at leaders' grand gestures against everyday grind, like turning international summits into market haggling. Structure-wise, I flowed associatively: from handshakes to "loading" airtime (tying into progress illusions), then hunger as "empty pots," energy as unreliable "tease," and so on to faith and unity. It's like a Lagos danfo route—twisty but purposeful, ending on hope to avoid pure cynicism. For Nigerian patterns, I leaned into rhetorical questions ("Shey you sabi?") and vivid similes (promises expiring like sachet water), but I twisted them fresh—no overdone "like say" loops from my history. Pidgin dominated for rhythm and warmth—it's how we yarn in the streets—but I slipped in standard English for punchlines, like describing tweets that "scatter everywhere," to keep it accessible without losing flavor. Balance was key; too much Pidgin feels forced, too little, unauthentic.

Cultural authenticity was my anchor—I wrote for Naija folks who nod at references like Onitsha bargaining or NEPA's two-hour tease because they've lived it. Jollof's cousin, Christmas rice, or herder-farmer clashes? They're effective hooks; they evoke shared frustration and warmth, turning news into "our story." I adapted events by localizing: Trump's walls become bandit barriers, foreign aid twists into practical "weave baskets" advice. It fits our sensibilities—humor as survival tool, laughing at pain without dismissing it.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the refinery punchline: politicians "chopping the pot first" before unity feasts. It works because it's visual, ties division to greed, and lands with a relatable "abi?"—inviting the audience in. The camel-holding-water bit for batteries? Absurd yet spot-on for our arid north. I toyed with unemployment stats from the background but ditched it; too broad, risked overlapping my old government rants. To dodge past patterns, I skipped callbacks like "speaking of" chains and banned phrases like "wetin dey happen"—instead, new bridges like "shift gear small." No exaggeration overload; focused on grounded hyperbole.

Gospel integration flowed organically as resilience threads—manna for hunger aid, loaves for fuel miracles—because faith's woven into our fabric, especially in crises. I placed them at transitions (end of energy, refinery sections) to pivot from despair to hope, never preachy; it's "if only leaders could multiply" as wry wish, not sermon. Strategy: Mirror scripture to everyday hustle, making it feel like wise uncle talk, not altar call.

From past scripts, I learned to evolve—my November ones were punchier but repetitive on Tinubu; this one's broader, fresher angles like drummers symbolizing endurance. Next time, I'd tighten transitions for snappier pacing; some yarns meandered. This script evolves my voice: deeper satire with unshakeable faith undertones, turning comedian into storyteller who heals through hilarity. Word count: 548. Onward.
