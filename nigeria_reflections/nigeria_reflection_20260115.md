# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2026-01-15

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here in my small Lagos flat with a cup of Milo cooling off beside me, I take a deep breath and look back at this script I just penned. It's like peering into the mirror after a long stage show – raw, revealing, and a bit humbling. I wrote this not just to make people laugh, but to capture the chaotic pulse of Naija life right now, blending the news with that sharp wit that keeps us sane. Let me break it down, step by step, like dissecting a stubborn plantain for boiling.

First, on content selection and prioritization: The context thrown my way was a jumble – political switches, Trump's bluster, doctors' strikes, Dangote's deals, and even lighter bits like Igbos in Lagos or unemployment talks. I zeroed in on the political defections (Bayelsa governor jumping to APC, northern governor eyeing Atiku) because they scream absurdity in Nigerian politics, like a Nollywood plot twist that's too real. Trump's threat stood out as comedically ripe – it's external drama clashing with our internal wahala, perfect for exaggeration. I emphasized these over, say, the World Bank meeting or Sanwo-Olu's coexistence speech because they had that high-stakes, shareable energy; people are buzzing about party-hopping and foreign meddling on WhatsApp groups. The doctors' strike and Dangote refinery got solid airtime for their everyday impact – fuel prices hit wallets, strikes hit health – but I kept IBB's birthday or unemployment seminars brief, as they felt like side dishes to the main stew. Prioritization came from gut feel: what ties into the "musical chairs" theme of instability without overwhelming the flow?

My comedic strategy was mostly satirical observational humor, poking at the ridiculousness of power plays while grounding it in everyday Naija struggles. I structured it as a loose stream-of-consciousness rant, starting with local politics, weaving to international noise, then circling back to economic pains and faith – like a market woman haggling from one stall to the next, building momentum with transitions like "speaking of dancing around issues." I drew on Nigerian patterns like pidgin metaphors (politics as football or Nollywood) because they're relatable and punchy; they mirror how we yarn on the street. For balance, I leaned 70% into pidgin for authenticity – words like "wahala" or "siddon" flow naturally in comedy – but slipped in standard English for biblical nods, making it accessible without losing flavor. Why? Pidgin amps the energy, standard smooths the satire.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted laughs that echo in a beer parlor, not a sterile hall. References like agbada, danfo queues, or NEPA light fickleness pull from shared Naija experiences – they're effective because they evoke that "ehn, true talk" nod. I adapted events like Trump's threat by Naija-fying it: diplomats explaining with "fuel and naira" instead of tanks, fitting our resilient, humorous take on threats. No forced Americanism; it's all filtered through our lens of surviving bigger messes.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the Trump punchline about building a wall with tanks – it flips his border obsession absurdly, landing because it's visual and ties back to our oil. The Solomon baby analogy for Tinubu's neutrality? Gold, as it cleverly nods to wisdom without overexplaining. I toyed with diving into "Igbos in Lagos" more, calling it a mirage, but pulled back; it risked stirring ethnic pots too hot for comedy, better as a quick jab. To dodge past patterns, I skipped overused callbacks or "you ever notice" setups, opting for fresh rule-of-three in party switches (queue, market stall, football halftime) and new phrases like "dust him agbada" instead of repeating "like say."

Weaving in gospel was deliberate but subtle – I aimed for organic hope amid chaos, not sermonizing. References like Jesus calming the sea or prodigal son prod the politicians' returns feel natural because they're proverbial in Naija storytelling, placed at emotional pivots (end of sections) to uplift without preaching, ending on faith as the steady light. It mirrors my voice: comedy with soul, reminding folks God dey in the mix.

From past scripts, I learned to vary energy – those Trump rants were heavy on exaggeration, so here I evolved by blending satire with quieter reflections, making my voice more layered, less shouty. Next time, I'd tighten transitions to avoid any drag in the Dangote bit. This one's growth: it's me maturing, turning news into not just laughs, but a mirror for healing our national headache.
