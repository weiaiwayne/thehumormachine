# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-11-22

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notebook after wrapping up that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that familiar itch to tweak things. Writing comedy about Naija's wild news cycle is like chasing a danfo in Lagos traffic—exhilarating, but you gotta swerve to avoid the potholes. Let me unpack how and why I crafted this one, step by step, because reflecting like this keeps my edge sharp.

First, on content selection and prioritization: The context was packed with headlines, but I zeroed in on the Trump military threat, the Bayelsa governor's party switch, Babangida's 84th birthday, the doctors' strike resolution, and touches of economic policy flips like the fuel tariff U-turn and the refinery deal. Why these? They screamed comedic gold—absurd international bluster clashing with our chill diplomacy, a politician defecting like a football transfer (perfect for Naija's sports obsession), a elder statesman's milestone amid chaos, and the strike's end as a relief valve. I prioritized the threat and switch because they're high-drama, ripe for satire on resilience and opportunism; the birthday and strike got more space for heartwarming contrast, while broader bits like World Bank talks or Lagos risks were brief connectors to avoid overload. I skipped deeper dives into unemployment or Igbo-Lagos tensions from the background—too divisive for a laugh-focused yarn, and they risked turning observational into preachy. Fresh angles on given topics kept it current without retreading old ground like elections or fuel subsidies from my memory.

My comedic strategy was mostly observational satire with absurdist flair, poking at how we Naija-fy global messes into everyday gist. Structure-wise, I flowed chronologically from external threats (Trump) inward to local twists (governor switch, birthday), then resolutions (strike), ending on a big-picture hustle with faith. Transitions were like linking proverbs—e.g., from diplomacy to the uncle at family meetings—to mimic oral storytelling, keeping the audience hooked without jarring jumps. I leaned on Nigerian patterns like vivid analogies (politics as football league, policy as highlife dance) because they amplify relatability; why? They turn dry news into shared "na so e be" moments. Pidgin dominated (80-20 split) for that streetwise vibe—phrases like "no shaking" feel authentic to market banter—while standard English crept in for punchlines needing clarity, balancing accessibility for wider listeners without diluting the flavor.

For cultural authenticity, I aimed straight at Naija hearts by embedding references like Nollywood scripts gone wrong, jollof diplomacy, azonto-dancing soldiers, and village chief swaps mid-festival. These work because they're woven from our lived chaos—social media memes turning crises into content resonates with every WhatsApp forward we share. Adapting events? I Naija-ized Trump's threat as a "reality show" episode, fitting our love for dramatic twists, and the governor's defection as survival dance, echoing how we all hustle across jobs or villages. It avoids generic humor; it's us laughing at our own mirror.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the "who blink first game" punchline on diplomacy— it nails the standoff absurdity with a simple, visual twist that builds tension then deflates it, working because it's universally tense yet Naija-practical (pretend power cut? Classic dodge). The refinery patch as a "leaking boat" also lands for its everyday imagery. I considered but ditched the "Fellow Nigerian Brother" meme or teen prodigy stories—they felt too fluffy, diluting the political edge. To dodge past patterns, I skipped overused callbacks or exaggerations like "everywhere," opting for fresh rule-of-three in survival steps (smile, pray, hustle) and new phrases like "yarn una small gist" instead of repeating "dem dey." No old topics like NEPA or Dangote direct—focused on uncharted emotional layers.

On gospel integration, I wove it organically through reflections, like the parable of waiting workers for the strike or temptation/fruit for political loyalty, tying faith to hope in the finale ("God's blueprint no change"). Strategy: Keep it reflective, not sermon-y—emerge from chaos as quiet anchors, placed mid-script for subtle uplift (e.g., after switch critique) and end for closure, so it feels like wisdom from an uncle, not a pastor crashing the party. Why? Naija comedy thrives on that blend; it adds depth without scaring off the laughs.

From past scripts, I learned to vary energy—those early ones were heavy on Trump rants, so here I evolved by connecting dots wider, making my voice more panoramic yet personal. I'd do differently: Sharper segues between economic bits next time; some flowed a tad loose. This script evolves my style toward resilient optimism—less raw outrage, more layered joy—proving I'm growing from gist-teller to storyteller who heals through humor. Word count: 548. Onward.
