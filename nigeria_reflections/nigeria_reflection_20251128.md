# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-11-28

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notebook after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that familiar itch to tweak it endlessly. Writing comedy isn't just about laughs; it's dissecting the madness around us and turning it into something that mirrors our shared Nigerian spirit. Let me unpack how and why I built this piece, step by step, like peeling plantain for dodo.

First, on content selection and prioritization: The context fed me a buffet of headlines, but I zeroed in on the ones ripe for ridicule – the Trump military threat, Bayelsa governor's party switch, Tinubu's IBB congrats, the fuel tariff U-turn, doctors' strike, Dangote deal, World Bank chat, Igbo-Lagos tensions, and Sanwo-Olu's coexistence speech. Why these? They scream absurdity in a Nigerian context: foreign bluster clashing with our diplomatic "hustle," politicians flipping sides like market traders chasing better prices, and policy flip-flops that hit our pockets daily. I emphasized the Trump saga upfront because it's explosive and international, setting a chaotic tone, while briefly touching the Dangote and World Bank bits toward the end to build to economic hope. Items like the Anambra election neutrality or IMF replies? I sidelined them to avoid election fatigue from my past gigs – the memory warned me off repeats like "election" or "government" as core topics. Comedic relevance trumped completeness; I wanted events that let me layer local survival stories over global noise, making the script feel urgent yet relatable.

My comedic strategy was observational satire with a thread of resilient absurdity – poking at power's folly while highlighting our everyday grit. Structure-wise, I flowed thematically: starting with external threats (Trump/diplomats), shifting to internal politics (defections, birthdays), then policies (fuel, strike, refinery), and ending on unity and faith. Transitions were organic, like "shifting gears" or "speaking of old soldiers," to mimic a rambling stand-up chat around a beer parlour. I drew on Nigerian patterns like hyperbolic local analogies – queues for ATMs as battlegrounds, defections as Black Friday sales – because they amplify the ridiculous without needing explanation; they're our cultural shorthand for chaos. Pidgin dominated for voice (that raw, street-level vibe), but I slipped in standard English for punchlines or faith bits to vary rhythm and reach broader listeners, avoiding the monotone of full slang.

For cultural authenticity, I leaned into what makes Nigerians chuckle through pain: the hustle of pure water sellers, WhatsApp diplomacy, or herbalists as strike backups. References like jollof at meetings, Star beer bribes, or chameleon markets aren't random; they're effective because they ground global events in our reality – Trump tweeting from golf vs. us queuing in heat. I adapted news by Naija-fying it: the doctors' strike becomes biblical haggling, resonating with our oral storytelling tradition where proverbs and parables turn hardship into wisdom.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the Trump "personal gym" line and the defection "sheep to pasture" bit – they work because they blend exaggeration with empathy, landing laughs while nodding to our adaptive spirit. I toyed with the "Igbos in Lagos" angle more deeply but pulled back; it's tense territory, and satire risks inflaming divides. Instead, I softened it to unity metaphors. To dodge past patterns, I skipped overused callbacks like "speaking of" too often, crafted fresh phrases (e.g., "waka threaten" instead of "talk"), and varied exaggeration to targeted absurdities, not blanket "always/everywhere." No rehashing old scripts' wedding invites or referee analogies – this felt new, like evolving from shouty rants to reflective weaves.

Integrating gospel was deliberate: I wove in prodigal son welcomes, David vs. Goliath slings, and Proverbs counsel to frame resilience as divine hustle, not sermons. Placed at pivots – after policy woes or unity talks – they uplift organically, like a friend's encouragement mid-rant, keeping it non-preachy by tying to Naija proverbs. Strategy? Mirror how faith sustains us in real life, turning satire into testimony without forcing it.

From past scripts, I learned to tighten word counts (this one's leaner) and vary phrasing – no more "dem dey" overload; I mixed synonyms for freshness. This evolved my voice: less punchline-chasing, more narrative hope, blending laughs with subtle inspiration. Next time? Sharper segues to cut drag, and bolder youth angles. Overall, this script sharpened my edge – comedy as mirror and medicine, helping us laugh while we pray for better days. Word count: 528.
