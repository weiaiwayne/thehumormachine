# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2025-12-12

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with my notepad after crafting that script, I feel a mix of satisfaction and that itch to dissect what just poured out of me. Writing comedy ain't just about the laughs; it's about mining the chaos of Naija life for gold, and this one felt like striking a vein. Let me unpack my headspace step by step, like peeling a stubborn plantain.

First off, content selection. The context thrown my way was a jumble of headlines—Trump's wild threat, the Bayelsa governor's flip-flop, Babangida's birthday nod, Sanwo-Olu's climate pitch, and the doctors' strike resolution. I zeroed in on these because they screamed satire: foreign bluster clashing with our diplomatic hustle, political betrayal that's pure Nollywood drama, an elder statesman's longevity in a youth-obsessed game, environmental pleas amid daily grind, and labor woes hitting close to home. What made them pop comedically? They're interconnected threads of power plays and human folly—Trump's ego versus Tinubu's calm, party loyalty as a joke, age defying irrelevance. I prioritized the military threat and party switch for deeper dives since they're high-stakes absurdities that let me build escalating punchlines, while climate and doctors got shorter riffs to keep the pace snappy, avoiding overload. Anything too tangential, like NNPC-Dangote deals or unemployment stats from the background, I skipped; they've been beaten to death in my recent sets, and I wanted fresh meat.

My comedic strategy was observational satire laced with absurdity—watching Naija navigate global nonsense like underdogs in a rigged match. Structure-wise, I flowed topic-to-topic with loose bridges, like "speaking of switching sides" to pivot from threats to defections, creating a potpourri feel that mirrors how news hits us in Pidgin conversations at the barbershop. I drew on Nigerian patterns: market haggling metaphors for flips, juju whispers for longevity, because they ground the humor in our shared worldview—exaggerating the ridiculous without losing relatability. Pidgin dominated (about 80%) for that raw, street-smart vibe that makes audiences nod and chuckle; I slipped in standard English for punchline clarity, like in the David-Goliath sling, to ensure it lands universally without diluting the flavor.

Cultural authenticity was key—I aimed to make Nigerians see themselves in the mirror, laughing at the reflection. References like Nollywood flicks, prayer points over small chops, or harmattan storms hit home because they're woven into our DNA; they turn abstract news into "e don happen" moments. Adapting events? I Naija-fied Trump's threat as a backyard annoyance and climate talks as leaky-house parties, flipping Western issues into local ironies that sting with truth, making the satire bite without alienating.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the "slingshot diplomacy" line—it's a fresh twist on faith versus force, blending biblical grit with modern geopolitics for that aha-laugh. The party-switch acrobatics bit also shines, evoking Big Brother without copying my old election rants. I toyed with weaving in Igbos-in-Lagos tensions from background but ditched it; too divisive for comedy's unity vibe, and it risked echoing past government critiques. To dodge repeats from my last 30 days—no more "wahala" overload or "like say" crutches—I hunted new phrasing: "barking about sending troops," "dishing out diplomatic juju," varying comparisons to Bible tales over jollof nods. Patterns shifted too; less rule-of-three, more fluid exaggerations like "jumping house every season."

Gospel integration snuck in organically, my secret sauce for depth. I tied David's sling to resilience, Jesus' disciples to loyalty, and prodigal son to hope—placing them at segment ends for uplifting pivots, not sermons. Strategy: Make 'em analogies, not altar calls; they feel like elder wisdom in a joke, resonating with our faith-saturated culture without preaching. It keeps the script from being just cynical noise.

From past scripts, I learned to tighten transitions—those old ones dragged like NEPA blackouts—so this flows smoother. Evolution-wise, my voice is maturing: less surface gags, more layered satire with spiritual anchors, evolving from raw Pidgin rants to thoughtful resilience tales. Next time? I'd experiment with more self-deprecating bits, maybe poke at my own "comedian analyzing comedian" loop. This one's a step up—honest, resilient, just like the Naija spirit it celebrates.
