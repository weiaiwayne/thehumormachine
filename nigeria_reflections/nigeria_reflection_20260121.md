# Meta-Cognitive Reflection - 2026-01-21

**By Chijioke Eze**

---

As Chijioke Eze, sitting here with a cup of herbal tea cooling off beside me, I find myself dissecting the script I just penned. It's like peeling back the layers of a fresh plantain to see what makes it ripe for the frying pan. This reflection isn't for the stage—it's my quiet space to unpack the how and why, to sharpen my craft without the spotlight.

First, on content selection and prioritization: The context fed me a buffet of Nigerian headlines, from Trump's military threats to refinery deals and Sanwo-Olu's climate pitch. I zeroed in on the political drama because it pulses with absurdity—diplomats summoned like errant family members, a governor flipping parties mid-stream, doctors striking while patients queue up. These felt comedically ripe; they're everyday chaos that Nigerians nod at with weary smiles. I prioritized the Trump threat and party switch as anchors because they're high-stakes national embarrassments that lend themselves to satire, while briefly touching Babangida's birthday and the refinery buzz for a lighter wrap-up. Less urgent items like Igbos in Lagos or unemployment reports got sidelined—too heavy or repetitive for a comedy flow; they risk turning laughs into lectures.

My comedic strategy was observational satire, painting current events as a chaotic Nollywood plot. I structured it like a meandering village yarn: starting with the foreign threat for a bang, transitioning via thematic links—like from diplomacy to political flips (both about shaky handshakes)—then easing into strikes, climate hopes, and elder celebrations. This creates a rhythmic flow, building from tension to release. For Nigerian patterns, I leaned on hyperbole in everyday scenarios (e.g., hospitals as "ghost towns") and ironic twists, but varied from past scripts by avoiding overused callbacks or exaggerations like "everywhere." Pidgin English dominated for warmth and rhythm—"e get as e be," "shebi"—to mimic street banter, blending with standard English for punchlines that land universally, keeping it accessible without diluting the flavor.

Cultural authenticity was key; I wanted humor that hits home for Naija folks scrolling news feeds. References like garri mornings, pounded yam diplomacy, and okada riders dodging floods ground it in our lived reality—effective because they evoke shared frustrations, turning global events into local gossip. I adapted stories to our sensibilities by framing politics as "musical chairs" or market haggling, which resonates with our oral tradition of witty proverbs, making the satire feel like elder wisdom laced with mockery.

Creatively, I'm proudest of the bull-handshake line for calming the "raging bull" of threats—it's visual, absurd, and ties diplomacy's futility perfectly. The pancake flip for the governor's defection works because it's domestic, relatable chaos. I toyed with the World Bank meeting but dropped it; too dry, overlapping with past economic jabs. To dodge repetition, I scanned my history—no direct echoes of "wahala" or Tinubu overload—and freshened phrases, opting for "scatter plans" over old standbys.

Weaving gospel elements was deliberate: I integrated faith organically as a resilient undercurrent, not sermons—phrases like "divine intervention holds steady" or Paul's Damascus switch pop up post-chaos to offer hope, mirroring how many Nigerians pray through headlines. Placed at transitions and closings, they provide emotional anchors without preachiness, reflecting my belief that comedy heals when it points upward subtly.

From past scripts, I learned to vary transitions—last ones felt list-like, so here I linked via human elements like "us at home." Next time, I'd tighten the refinery bit; it trails off. This script evolves my voice: more layered, blending satire with subtle uplift, maturing from raw rants to reflective resilience. It's growth—laughter with depth, just like life in Naija.
